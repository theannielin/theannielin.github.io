1700

Pre-Settlement Forest: A Varied Landscape
In the pre-settlement forest, natural variation across sites and ongoing natural and human disturbance processes led to differences in age, density, size, and species of trees across a wide range of sites. Notice the large trees and large fallen trunks on the right in the diorama. Compare them with the smaller, younger trees on the left.

1740

An Early Settler Clears a Homestead
For most of the New England region, European settlement occurred largely during the 18th century. Through forest clearing, hunting, and trapping, the abundance of many species changed rapidly and the wilderness was gradually transformed into a domesticated rural landscape.

1830

Height of Forest Clearing and Agriculture
The peak of deforestation and agricultural activity across most of New England occurred from 1830 to 1880. Across much of New England, 60 to 80 percent of the land was cleared for pasture, tillage, orchards and buildings. Small remaining areas of woodland were subjected to frequent cuttings for lumber and fuel.

1850

Farm Abandonment
Beginning in the mid-1800s and continuing for more than a century, farming declined on a broad scale across New England. Abandoned pastures and fields rapidly developed into forests. In central Massachusetts and across much of central New England these forests were dominated by white pines.

1910

"Old-Field" White Pine Forest on Abandoned Farmland
As the "old-field" stands of white pine reached middle age, it became evident that they contained a valuable and rapidly growing crop of second-growth timber. As this white pine became marketable portable sawmills appeared across central New England. One of the most common and valuable uses of these pines was for "box boards" used to make shipping containers prior to the development of corrugated cardboard in the 1930s.

1915

"Old-Field" White Pine is Succeeded by Hardwoods
Clear-cutting of the "old-field" white pines led to the succession of mixed hardwoods across much of the landscape. The inability of white pine to sprout after being cut, in contrast to the prolific sprouting of our hardwood species, facilitated this succession. Patterns of succession enhanced the diversity of forest types, providing a wide range of wildlife habitats.

1930

A Vigorously Growing Forest of Hardwoods
One of the characteristic features of the hardwood forest that developed after the clear-cutting of the "old-field" white pines is the predominance of multi-stemmed sprout clumps. Fast-growing species that sprout prolifically -- red oak, red maple, white ash, birches, and black cherry -- are strongly represented. Red oaks are just beginning to overtop the other trees in this view.

Now

The Modern Forest Landscape
In the period since the dioramas were constructed, the trends in forest development illustrated in the 1930 model have continued. Remarkable expanses of maturing forest extend across a densely populated landscape in the northeastern United States.

Forest-Atmosphere Exchange

The thick forest cover of the pre-settlement era prevented erosion by intercepting rain and recycling moisture to the atmosphere through evapotranspiration.

Biodiversity

Because of various natural and human disturbances, there was a wide range of vegetation and wildlife in the pre-settlement forest. Throughout the transitional forest zone of New England, you were able to find “northern species” (in the cooler, wetter part of the forest zone) such as hemlock, beech, and sugar maple as well as “southern species” (in more exposed, drier sites) such as white pine, red oak, and white ash. These plants supported a myriad of animal species, such bobolinks, meadowlarks, beavers, wolves, and bears.

History

The pre-settlement forest was a changing forest with considerable variation in wildlife and vegetation due to an ongoing process of natural disturbances (e.g. hurricanes, ice storms, pathogens, and fires ignited by lightning). Before settlement, most of the forest was not majorly affected by human interference, leading to a mosaic of different forests.

Land Use

The pre-settlement forest was not undisturbed by humans: Native Americans cleared forests to support their villages and fields and burned tracts of forest land to improve hunting. Additionally, European settlers were trickling in to explore the opportunities offered by the diverse habitats of New England.

Soil

Assorted soil types were found throughout the land, including sandy, droughty soils, wetter till soils, and shallow soils with bedrock outcrops. This range allowed for a variety of plant and animal life to develop in the pre-settlement forest.

Water

Sites flooded by beavers and areas inundated by annual fluctuations in the water table supported herbaceous/shrub vegetation (in contrast to surrounding forests). As a result, the pre-settlement landscape was very diverse.

History

European settlement occurred largely during the 18th century, with many settlers coming from “frontier” towns farther east (such as Rutland, Lancaster, Lunenburg, and Brookfield). The Colonial government granted land to proprietors for them to establish settlements in the frontier wilderness. For the first year or two, the settlers often traveled between their eastern homesteads and the forest, moving back and forth seasonally to clear as much land as possible before winter.

Land Use

Many trees were cut down by settlers to build their dwellings, use for fuel/fertilizer, and make fences. Land was cleared so that there would be space to plant crops and raise farm animals.

Soil

To determine soil quality for their farms, settlers often inspected forest composition for clues. They noted, for example, that sugar maple, beech, and ash grew on productive site suitable for crops, while hemlock and red maple sites were usually growing on wetter rockier soil less suitable for farming. The settlers also suffused charcoal, potash, and forest leaf litter into the soil to act as fertilizers.

History

During this height of forest clearing and agriculture, a thriving economy was established based on widespread local and home industry. The landscape dominated by human activity with human populations becoming fairly spread out. Labor transformed the forest landscape into rural farmlands.

Land Use

During this time period, 60 to 80 percent of the land was cleared. Most cleared land was for grazing animals while less than 10 percent was actively plowed for crops. With little woodland left, there was a great demand for wood. (Wood was too valuable and less durable for fencing, so stone was used instead.) The most desirable trees were claimed for lumber while the least desirable trees were burned as fuel.

Biodiversity

As farmers abandoned their livelihoods, abandoned fields and pastures pastures sprouted up across New England. They then rapidly developed into forests of white pine, which were able to spread so quickly because their seeds were less palatable to animals than other hardwoods (such as red maple, white ash, red oak, chestnut, and gray and paper birch). The new pine grew very thick over the next several years, and allowed little undergrowth to develop. Deer, beaver, and bear populations were still low during this time, but meadowlarks and bobolinks prospered.

History

Farming declined on a broad scale across New England from the mid-1800s to more than a century later. The Industrial Revolution, a better transportation system, the discovery of gold in California, and the Civil War all contributed to drawing people away from rural regions. However, urban and suburban areas continued to grow in concentrated populations.

Water

For rain to become groundwater, it must seep through soil into the water table. Trees help this process because the soil and materials underneath help to hold moisture for replenishing ground water levels. With the return of white pines to the previous rural landscape, a lesser amount of water was running off into streams and lakes; it was kept underground instead.

Biodiversity

With the removal of pine for commercial use, sprouts and saplings of oak, black birch, chestnut, and red maple were finally able to outgrow the new pine. Bear, deer, and beaver populations were also slowly beginning to recover.

History

Because the white pine made excellent framing material, finish lumber, and other products (in a time before cardboard and plastics), the trees became so marketable that portable sawmills and new wood-using factories began appearing across New England. A farmer who owned a 100-acre-wood lot could make $30,000, which was a huge sum to make. Between 1890 and 1910 (peak of the timber boom), about 15 billion board-feet of second growth white pine (with a manufacture value of over $400 million) was cut in central New England.

To gather the valuable white pine trees, clear-cutting and hauling with horses was facilitated by felling the trees in strips and windrowing the slash into long lines that were burned or left to rot. The logs were then transported to portable steam sawmills, where they were sawed into boards.

Biodiversity

After the white pine fell from logging, hardwoods began sprouting. These included red maple, red and white oak, white ash, chestnut, black cherry, and black birch. Across the central new England landscape, a varied habitats developed, habitats that were suitable for species of open, grassy, and shrubby areas as well as forest edges. History

In the early 1860’s, Henry Thoreau noted the progression from open field to pine forest and then the sudden change to young hardwood forest after the pine was cut. He called this process “succession,” a term that foresters and ecologists still use today. As forests continued to expand over former agricultural land, the New England landscape was poised for a return of its native wildlife.

History

In 100 years, much of the New England countryside has gone from open agricultural land to largely closed woodland and has supported two completely different types of forests. Both forests types resulted from the interaction of human activity, variation in the environment, and the biological characteristics of the native tree species. Without planning and with little encouragement, a resilient and dynamic new forest has developed across the region.

In this modern era, the forest has been subjected to novel impacts, including forms of air pollution (e.g. ozone, nitrogen oxides, sulfur dioxide). This pollution has had many different effects, affecting temperature and the health of forest life. However, the forest does offset some of this pollution through its natural processes.

Biodiversity

Since the creation of the Harvard Forest dioramas, more shade tolerant and long-lived species are thriving in New England forests. However, pests (such as gypsy moth, Dutch elm disease, beech bark disease, hemlock woolly adelgid) have impacted the growth of some of these trees. Some birds (e.g. bobolinks, meadowlarks, song sparrows, and quail) have seen a decline since the 1900’s. Meanwhile, woodland plants, beavers, fishers, bears, bobcats, moose, turkeys, pileated woodpeckers, and eagles have become more common across the landscape.

History

As these forests grow and mature and as dead and decaying wood accumulates on the ground, the forest landscape becomes increasingly natural in appearance and character. With time, too, early successional species decline and more shade-tolerant and long-lived species increase. Still, the legacy of land-use history persists in the distribution of species and the often abrupt transition between forest types. The stonewalls serve as a constant reminder of this land-use history.

The chemistry of the soil can be affected by events such as acid rain, which can cause the soil to become toxic to some plants. Water

Water pollution has also posed a significant dilemma for the modern forest. Runoff (for example, nitrogen runoff) from urban environments and farms can affect life in downstream areas. Policy now plays an important role in regulating the water of the modern age.
